Interview with márton árva

on cinematic machines

Márton Árva is a film freak, critic, and Film Studies PhD candidate, venturing to explore alternatives to mainstream Western aesthetics, mostly from a decolonial or postcolonial standpoint. He was recently working with the school and took part in our online workshops and then proposed a brilliant course of his own: Cinematic Machines.

What are you most excited about in teaching the upcoming course?

I think I have found some quite interesting recurrent motifs, and dialogues between films that I’m excited to share. Besides, I will be very curious about our group discussions regarding both well-known titles and hidden treasures of film history. I would like us to draw the contours of some ideas, get to know important discourses, and highlight ways to approach issues of film and technology. But I would also like to explore some less straightforward issues, or more controversial films together with the group. In this, I’m looking forward to getting to know the standpoints of the attendees, who will surely propose some different perspectives to look at things, and will bring up film examples that I haven’t even heard about.

What was my motivation in creating it?

I wanted to share hopes and fears concerning technology, as well as to gather a group of like-minded film enthusiasts, whose imagination is also catalyzed by powerful stories and images, but who are still willing to look behind these immediate effects, and find ways to contextualize and analyze films. Discussions about cinema are infinitely rich and everyone can come up with relevant experiences, contributing in very valuable ways. So what I would like to do more with my introductory presentations and questions, is to trigger the fantasy and creativity of people.

Why do you think this course is relevant now?

It is just impressive how influential and ubiquitous moving images are, and I think we should at least try to build up some kind of an overview and conceptual toolkit that helps us to formulate our own takes with regard to the stories they tell us and the emotions they provoke. Although film is no longer the only, not even the most popular form of audiovisual storytelling, it has certainly been one of the most powerful one in articulating the mythology of modernity and technology. In this sense, it is exactly now, when we are progressively abandoning cinemas either because of the risk of contagion or out of curiosity to try shorter, more immersive, or simply more recent audiovisual experiences, that we need to address and question this modern cinematic mythology. Because this mythology still shapes our life and thoughts every day, even if we now know a lot about the harm it does to arrange things in rational ways and to seek dominance and control over our environment. I think it is very useful to reflect on the images and narratives that build up this cultural imaginary, and ask how they work.
